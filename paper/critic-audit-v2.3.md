## 3. STRUCTURE & FLOW

### Overall Assessment: **Solid but Too Long and Repetitive**

The paper tells a coherent story with a clear four-part structure:
1. **Validation** (TC1–5): Can ASTRA recover known results? ✅
2. **Discovery** (TC6): Can ASTRA discover from data alone? ✅
3. **Live Deployment** (§7): Does it work on real data at scale? ✅
4. **Cross-Domain** (§8): Does it generalize beyond astrophysics? ✅

This is a good narrative arc. However:

#### P1 Issues

- **The paper is too long for RASTI.** At ~12,000 words / 22 pages with 2 tables and 6 figures, this exceeds typical RASTI article length (8–15 pages). The cross-domain section (§8) alone adds ~5 pages.
- **The Conclusion (§10) is essentially a complete restatement of all results.** At ~80 lines, it repeats every test case in bullet form. A conclusion should synthesize, not recapitulate. Cut to ~30 lines: state the main contribution (integrated framework + causal reasoning + cross-domain generalization), note what's novel vs. validation, and point to future work. Move the detailed result summaries to an appendix or just reference the relevant sections.

#### P2 Issues

- **6-test-case structure is effective but risks monotony.** TC1–TC5 each follow the same pattern: Objective → Data → Results → Interpretation → Limitations. This is clear but repetitive. Consider: (a) grouping TC1 and TC5 (same dataset), (b) condensing TC2 and TC4 (both "basic demonstration" by the author's own admission).
- **§8 (Cross-Domain) adds genuine value** but feels somewhat bolted on. The transition from astrophysics to economics in one paragraph (line 930–933) is abrupt. Add a bridge paragraph explaining why cross-domain testing matters for validating the framework's claims of generality. Currently this justification appears in the Discussion (§9.3) — move it earlier, before the results.
- **§7.7 (Galaxy Survey Discovery Mode) doesn't belong in "Live System Validation"** — it uses simulated data "drawn from SDSS scaling relations," not real archival data. This is a validation test, not a live deployment. Move to a new section or append to TC3/TC6.
- **Discussion is balanced and honest** — one of the paper's strengths. The "What ASTRA Does Not Claim" subsection and the "ASTRA's Role in Scientific Discovery" subsection are well-written and show appropriate epistemic humility.

## 8. WHAT THE PAPER DOES WELL

To be fair, this paper has several notable strengths:

1. **Epistemic honesty is exceptional.** The repeated acknowledgment that TC1–5 recover known results, that TC6 uses synthetic data, and that the live deployments are "not novel discoveries" shows scientific maturity rare in AI-for-science papers.
2. **The Phillips Curve failure and FDR attrition** are highlighted rather than buried — this is exactly what reviewers want to see.
3. **The four modes of evidence** framework is a genuinely useful contribution to how such systems should be validated.
4. **Cross-domain generalization** testing is the right approach and distinguishes this from papers that overfit to their home domain.
5. **The causal reasoning emphasis** (TC4, TC6) addresses a real gap — most ML papers stop at correlation.
6. **Limitations sections are thorough** — each test case honestly states what it does and doesn't demonstrate.
7. **Reproducibility provisions** are strong — notebooks, scripts, Example1–6, and a public repository.
