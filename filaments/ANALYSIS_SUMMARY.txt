ANSWER SUMMARY
================================================================================

The most compelling explanation is the **SONIC SCALE OF TURBULENT CASCADE**

The sonic scale (λ_sonic) is the scale in supersonic turbulence where the
turbulent velocity dispersion equals the thermal sound speed. This represents
a fundamental transition:
  • Large scales (> λ_sonic): Supersonic turbulence, shocks dominate
  • Small scales (< λ_sonic): Subsonic turbulence, acoustic waves dominate

For typical molecular cloud conditions:
  λ_sonic ≈ 0.1 pc

This scale sets the MINIMUM width of filaments because:
  1. Filaments form preferentially at shocks in the supersonic regime
  2. The sonic scale sets the thickness of post-shock regions
  3. Below the sonic scale, shocks cannot form (insufficient velocity contrast)

Why is it density-independent?
  • The sonic scale depends on LARGE-SCALE turbulent properties
    (injection scale, energy cascade rate), not local density
  • Local density variations occur WITHIN the turbulent cascade but do
    not significantly affect the sonic scale location
  • Temperature variations are small (5-20 K) compared to density
    variations (10² - 10⁶ cm⁻³)

Confidence in this explanation: ~90%

THE 0.1 PC MYSTERY EXPLAINED
================================================================================

The characteristic 0.1 pc width of interstellar filaments is one of the most
remarkable examples of scale-invariance in astrophysics. The leading
explanation—the sonic scale of turbulent cascade—represents a triumph of
theoretical astrophysics: a fundamental physical scale that can be calculated
from first principles and matches observations with remarkable precision.

This scale emerges from the fundamental physics of supersonic turbulence:
the transition where turbulent velocity dispersion equals the thermal sound
speed. It represents the smallest scale at which shocks can form, and thus
sets the minimum width for shock-generated filaments.

The fact that this width is approximately constant across 5 orders of
magnitude in density and diverse environments tells us something profound
about the nature of interstellar turbulence: the large-scale turbulent
cascade, not local conditions, sets the characteristic scale for density
structure.

Yet mysteries remain, and the filament width problem continues to drive both
observational and theoretical research in star formation and interstellar
medium physics.
